### **Abstract**

Reinforcement Learning (RL) has enabled Large Language Models (LLMs) to acquire increasingly complex reasoning and agentic behaviors. In this work, we propose two simple techniques to improve policy gradient algorithms for LLMs. First, we replace the fixed anchor policy during RL with an Exponential Moving Average (EMA), similar to a target network in deep Q-learning. Second, we introduce Top-k KL estimator, which allows for flexible interpolation between exact KL and sampled KL. We derive the stability conditions for using EMA anchor; moreover, we show that our Top-k KL estimator yields both unbiased KL values and unbiased gradients at any k, while bringing the benefits of exact KL. When combined with GRPO, the two techniques (EMA-PG) lead to a significant performance boost. On math reasoning, it allows R1-distilled Qwen-1.5B to reach 53.9% on OlympiadBench compared to 50.8% by GRPO. On agentic RL domains, with Qwen-3B base, EMA-PG improves GRPO by an average of 33.3% across 7 datasets of Q&A with search engines, including  $29.7\% \rightarrow 44.1\%$  on HotpotQA,  $27.4\% \rightarrow 40.1\%$  on 2WikiMultiHopQA. Overall, we show that EMA-PG is a simple, principled, and powerful approach to scaling RL for LLMs. Code: github.com/LunjunZhang/ema-pg.

### 2. Related Work

**Improving RL for LLMs** Leveraging verifiable domains such as math and coding, lots of effort has gone into scaling RL for reasoning (Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025) and agentic tasks (Kimi et al., 2025a; Comanici et al., 2025). The predominant algorithm for RL in reasoning, GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), has gained widespread

adoption due to its effectiveness. Many works have tried to further improve it, which include removing bias in policy gradient (Liu et al., 2025b), better importance weight clipping (Yu et al., 2025), using sequence-level objective (Zheng et al., 2025), asynchronous data generation (Piché et al., 2025), better scaling practices (Khatri et al., 2025). Since we exclusively focus on the KL component in RL, our work is largely orthogonal to those techniques above.

**KL Estimators for LLMs** KL estimation for LLMs is challenging due to autoregressive action space and large vocabulary size. Early work on RLHF adds a sequence-level KL penalty to the sequence-level reward (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a). As reasoning models begin to scale the sequence length of thinking traces, token-level KL regularization becomes popular (Guo et al., 2025). Token-level sampled KL estimators are typically K1, K2, and K3 (Schulman, 2020); K3 was used in GRPO. Since then, many works have pointed out that K3 gradients are forward KL gradients (Tang & Munos, 2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Shah et al., 2025). Sampled KL ignores the fact that we have white-box access to logits and next-token distributions; knowledge distillation has shown that such information is very useful to learning (Hinton et al., 2015; Kim & Rush, 2016; Agarwal et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). When the vocabulary is large, storing full logits is too memory-intensive; our insight is to reduce the memory usage of logits via Top-k truncation.

EMA in Deep Learning Deep O-learning almost universally uses an EMA network to produce stable learning targets (Mnih et al., 2015; Lillicrap et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018). Self-supervised learning has also used EMA for stable bootstrapping (He et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020b; Busbridge et al., 2023). EMA has been shown to improve generalization in deep learning (Nichol et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). In the diffusion model literature, EMA is often used to produce the final model (Ho et al., 2020a). For RL on LLMs, prior work has also suggested using an EMA anchor for alignment (Gorbatovski et al., 2024; Ramé et al., 2024); our work differs in how the KL is computed. Elastic reset (Noukhovitch et al., 2023) also keeps an EMA copy during RL, but does not use it for reference policy. The effectiveness of EMA can be partially explained by Linear Mode Connectivity (Frankle et al., 2020) and Monotonic Linear Interpolation (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Lucas et al., 2021) between neural networks that share the same initialization.

# 3. Background

Reinforcement Learning (RL) Given a pre-trained LLM, RL trains the model to maximize a reward function while remaining close to the original model. Concretely, let  $\pi_{\theta}$  denote a policy (language model) parameterized by  $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$ , and let R(x,y) be a reward function scoring response y to prompt x. The standard objective is to maximize:

<span id="page-2-2"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_{x} \{ \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} [R(x,y)] - \beta KL (\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \parallel \pi_{ref}(\cdot \mid x)) \}$$
 (1)

where  $x \sim \mathcal{D}$  is a sampled prompt,  $\pi_{\text{ref}}$  is the reference policy (also called the **anchor** policy),  $\beta$  controls how much  $\pi_{\theta}$  is allowed to deviate from the anchor. The term  $\text{KL}(\pi_{\theta} \| \pi_{\text{ref}}) = \mathbb{E}_x \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x)}[\log(\pi_{\theta}(y|x)/\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x))]$  is the reverse KL divergence for the policy  $\pi_{\theta}$ .

**Exponential Moving Average (EMA)** EMA is a technique for maintaining a smoothed version of an iteratively updated quantity. Given a sequence  $\{\theta_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty}$  and coefficient  $\eta \in (0,1)$ , the EMA sequence  $\{\theta_{\text{ema.}},t\}$  is:

$$\theta_{\text{ema, 0}} = \theta_0$$
  $\theta_{\text{ema, t+1}} = \eta \, \theta_{\text{ema, t}} + (1 - \eta) \, \theta_t$  (2)

which is widely used in deep Q-learning as target network.

**Local Quadratic Approximation of KL** The Fisher information matrix of a policy  $\pi_{\theta}$  is defined as:

$$F_{\theta} \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ (\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)) (\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x))^{\top} \right]$$

which captures the local sensitivity of a policy's output distribution to parameter changes. The natural gradient  $F_{\theta}^{-1}g$  is the steepest-ascent direction under a local KL trust-region constraint. For  $\theta' = \theta + \delta$  with  $\|\delta\| \to 0$ ,

<span id="page-2-1"></span>
$$KL(\pi_{\theta} \| \pi_{\theta+\delta}) = \delta^{\top} F_{\theta} \delta / 2 + O(\|\delta\|^3), \tag{3}$$

and the gradient w.r.t  $\theta$  is:  $\nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta} || \pi_{\theta'}) \approx F_{\theta}(\theta - \theta')$ .

### <span id="page-2-0"></span>4. Training Dynamics of the EMA Anchor

Consider the policy gradient  $g_t = (R \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta})|_{\theta = \theta_t}$  at t:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha [g_t - \beta \nabla_{\theta} KL(\pi_{\theta} || \pi_{\theta_{\text{ema}, t}})|_{\theta = \theta_t}]$$
  
$$\theta_{\text{ema, t+1}} = \eta \theta_{\text{ema, t}} + (1 - \eta) \theta_t$$

We define the *lag* between current and EMA weights as:

$$\delta_t := \theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema, }t} \tag{4}$$

Using the local approximation (3), with  $F_t = F_{\theta_t}$ 

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha g_t - \alpha \beta F_t \delta_t \tag{5}$$

The joint dynamics of  $\theta_t$  and  $\delta_t$  becomes:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \theta_{t+1} \\ \delta_{t+1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I & -\alpha\beta F_t \\ 0 & \eta I - \alpha\beta F_t \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \theta_t \\ \delta_t \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \alpha I \\ \alpha I \end{bmatrix} g_t \qquad (6)$$

**Lemma 4.1.** Quasi-steady state: If we assume that the gradient and the Fisher matrix change extremely slowly over k steps, meaning that for  $\Delta = 0 \cdots k - 1$ :

$$g_{t+\Delta} \approx g, \qquad F_{t+\Delta} \approx F \tag{7}$$

then, under the following definitions of matrices D,  $S_k$ ,  $M_k$ :

$$D = \eta I - \alpha \beta F \quad S_k = \sum_{\Delta=0}^{k-1} D^{\Delta} \quad M_k = \sum_{\Delta=0}^{k-1} S_{\Delta}$$

we have: firstly, I - D is invertible and  $S_k = (I - D^k)(I - D)^{-1}$ ,  $M_k = (kI - S_k)(I - D)^{-1}$ ; secondly, we have a closed-form dynamics of EMA Policy Gradient:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \theta_{t+k} \\ \delta_{t+k} \\ g \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I & -\alpha\beta F S_k & \alpha k I - \alpha^2 \beta F M_k \\ 0 & D^k & \alpha S_k \\ 0 & 0 & I \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \theta_t \\ \delta_t \\ g \end{bmatrix}$$

**Lemma 4.2.** Stability Condition: Let  $\{v_i\}_{i=0}^{d-1}$  be the eigenbasis of the Fisher matrix F, and  $\{\lambda_i\}_{i=0}^{d-1}$  be its corresponding eigenvalues (with  $\lambda_{\max}$  being the largest eigenvalue). The dynamics of the lag  $\delta_t$  in the eigenbasis of the Fisher, where  $\delta_t^{(i)} := \delta_t^\top v_i$  and  $g^{(i)} := g^\top v_i$ , can be written as:

$$\delta_{t+k}^{(i)} = \underbrace{(\chi_i)^k \delta_t^{(i)}}_{transient} + \underbrace{\alpha \frac{1 - (\chi_i)^k}{1 - \chi_i} g^{(i)}}_{particular \ solution} \quad \chi_i = \eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_i \quad (8)$$

*Thus, the stability condition of*  $\delta$  *is:* 

$$\alpha\beta\lambda_{\max} < 1 + \eta \tag{9}$$

### **Summary of Stability / Oscillation Conditions:**

- 1.  $0 < \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} < \eta$ : stable, non-oscillatory.
- 2.  $\eta < \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} < 1 + \eta$ : stable, oscillatory.
- 3.  $\alpha\beta\lambda_{\rm max} > 1 + \eta$ : unstable.

Which characterizes the exact conditions for stability in EMA-PG's training dynamics under mild assumptions (7).

# 5. KL Estimators: Which and Why

### <span id="page-3-0"></span>5.1. Sequence-level KL or Token-level KL?

Because LLMs have an autoregressive action space, it is worth discussing whether we should apply sequence-level KL or token-level KL. For a language model, the KL component in (1) can be rewritten as the loss:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_{\mathrm{seq}} \triangleq -\log(\pi_{\theta}(y|x)/\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y|x)) \tag{10}$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{seq}} \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} [-\mathrm{sg}(\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_{\mathrm{seq}}) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)] \quad (11)$$

<span id="page-3-1"></span>where  $sg(\cdot)$  denotes the stop gradient operator. Note that this is an unbiased **sequence-level KL** estimator:

<span id="page-3-2"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) || \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x)) = \nabla_{\theta} L_{\text{KL}}^{\text{seq}}(\theta)$$
 (12)

This KL regularizer was used in both for InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) and WARP (Ramé et al., 2024). It is simple to implement: just adding a scalar sequence logprob difference to the scalar sequence-level reward would suffice. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) also uses sequence-level KL.

In contrast, **token-level KL** regularization, as popularized by GRPO (Shao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025a), implements the following:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_{n} \triangleq \mathrm{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x, y_{0:n-1}) \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid x, y_{0:n-1})) \quad (13)$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{token}} \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} \left[ \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \nabla_{\theta} \widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_{n} \right]$$
 (14)

<span id="page-3-4"></span>where the KL estimator is a design choice (See Tab 1). We assume the use of exact KL rather than sampled KL in our analysis of token-level KL. Token-level KL was traditionally seen as a surrogate objective for sequence-level KL; here we analyze their differences. Rewrite sequence-level KL as:

$$\rho_n \triangleq \log(\pi_{\theta}(y_n|x, y_{0:n-1})/\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_n|x, y_{0:n-1}))$$

$$\omega_n \triangleq \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_n|x, y_{0:n-1})$$

It can be shown that (§D):

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{seq}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} \left[ \sum_{n} \left( \sum_{m \geq n} \rho_{m} \right) \omega_{n} \right]$$
 (15)

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\text{KL}}^{\text{token}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} \left[ \sum_{n} \left( \rho_{n} \right) \omega_{n} \right]$$
 (16)

The difference is clear: sequence-level KL punishes each current token for the divergence of all its future tokens until the end of sequence, while token-level KL does not.

We find that in reasoning and agentic settings, since the sequence lengths of thinking traces are long and the rewards are reliable, sequence-level KL does not help; **token-level KL should be used instead**. In other words, during RL training for reasoning, KL should be token-wise in the loss, not added to the outcome reward, even though sequence-level KL may seem more "correct" (12) for objective (1).

### <span id="page-4-0"></span>5.2. Exact KL or Sampled KL?

For token-level KL estimation, one can technically implement **exact KL**, which is unbiased and low-variance, but at the cost of memory. We use following notations:

$$h_n \triangleq (x, y_{0:n-1}), \quad \mathbb{E}_n[\cdot] \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{y_n \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | h_n)}[\cdot]$$
 (17)

In the case of reverse KL, for each token  $y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)$ :

$$KL_n \triangleq \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{ref}(j \mid h_n)}$$
 (18)

Since this is the ground-truth definition of reverse KL, it naturally yields exact values and exact gradients. The problem with exact KL is that it requires storing full logits for every position in each sequence both for current policy  $\pi_{\theta}$  and reference policy  $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ , costing a memory of  $O(|\mathcal{V}|)$  per token. The alternative, which has negligible memory footprint O(1), is **sampled KL**. For the n-th position in y, let

$$y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n) \quad w_n \triangleq \pi_{\text{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n) / \pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n) \quad (19)$$

The K1, K2, K3 estimators (Schulman, 2020) are given by:

$$(K1)_n \triangleq -\log w_n \quad (K2)_n \triangleq (1/2)(\log w_n)^2$$

$$(K3)_n \triangleq -\log w_n + w_n - 1$$

None of the existing sampled KL estimators simultaneously gives <u>both</u> unbiased value and unbiased gradient on the token level. Ideally, we would like an KL estimator  $\widehat{KL}_n$  to suffice both conditions (with notations from (17)):

<span id="page-4-2"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_n[\widehat{KL}_n] = KL_n \quad \mathbb{E}_n[\nabla_{\theta}\widehat{KL}_n] = \nabla_{\theta}KL_n \qquad (20)$$

K1 and K3 give the correct values but wrong gradients; K2 give the correct gradients but wrong values (see Tab 1). We show that K1 and K3 can be fixed with a simple trick. Let

<span id="page-4-4"></span>
$$r_n \triangleq \pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n) / \operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)) \tag{21}$$

where sg means stop gradient. We construct the following unbiased sampled KL estimators to satisfy (20):

<span id="page-4-3"></span>
$$(\mathbf{K3}^{++})_n \triangleq r_n(-\log w_n + w_n - 1)$$
 (22)

$$(\mathbf{K4})_n \triangleq r_n \cdot \operatorname{sg}(-\log w_n)$$
 (23)

<span id="page-4-1"></span>Eq (22) is named  $K3^{++}$  because of its resemblance to the modified K3 in (Liu et al., 2025a), but here we disentangle generic off-policy correction (which can be applied to any sampled KL estimator, not just K3; moreover, importance weights should be applied with  $sg(\cdot)$  on themselves) from an actual change in KL direction:

- $K3^{++}$  is obtained by multiplying existing K3 by  $r_n$  in (21). This simple change alone converts the gradient from forward KL to reverse KL.
- K4 is obtained by first doing stop gradient on K1 and then multiplying it by  $r_n$  in (21). It is simpler, and it is unbiased as well.

K3<sup>++</sup> and K4 satisfy the conditions in (20): they yield both unbiased value and unbiased gradient for reverse KL.

<span id="page-4-5"></span>In addition, the success of K3's forward KL gradients raises the interesting question of whether forward KL is just as effective as reverse KL in regularizing RL training dynamics. We propose a **forward KL estimator K5**, which produces unbiased forward KL values and unbiased forward KL gradients under  $\mathbb{E}_n[\cdot]$  (17) when sampling from (19):

$$(K5)_n \triangleq \operatorname{sg}(w_n) \log w_n + \log r_n \tag{24}$$

When sampling is off-policy  $(\pi_{\text{sampling}} \neq \pi_{\theta})$ , any sampled KL estimator  $\widehat{KL}_n$  above simply needs to multiply the (stop-gradient) importance weight  $\operatorname{sg}(\frac{\pi_{\theta}}{\pi_{\text{sampling}}})$  to remain unbiased.

<span id="page-5-2"></span>![](_page_5_Figure_1.jpeg)

Concretely, when  $y_n \sim \pi_{\text{sampling}}$ , any previously unbiased  $\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_n$  under  $y_n \sim \pi_\theta$  will require an off-policy correction  $\mathrm{KL}_n = \mathbb{E}_{y_n}[s_n\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_n]$  with importance weight (IW)  $s_n$ :

$$y_n \sim \pi_{\text{sampling}}(\cdot|h_n) \quad s_n \triangleq \operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(y_n|h_n)/\pi_{\text{sampling}}(y_n|h_n))$$

On the other hand, exact KL is always on-policy (but memory-intensive, with  $O(|\mathcal{V}|)$  rather than O(1) memory footprint), and thus does not require off-policy correction.

### <span id="page-5-0"></span>5.3. Top-k KL Estimator

We now aim to combine the best of both worlds of exact KL and sampled KL. We start from the observation that for modern LLMs, the probability mass tends to concentrate on a small number of top tokens at each generation step; it was for this reason that top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018) and nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019) were effective. The vocabulary size for modern LMs is very large (typically 100k-200k), so most of the logit indices in  $\mathcal V$  contribute very minimally to the KL value or KL gradients.

Our key insight is to **only partially compute exact KL on the top-k logit indices** of the policy under which the expectation is taken (which is current policy for reverse KL, reference policy for forward KL), and then **use a partially masked sampled KL to correct for the tail of the distribution**, so that we arrive at an unbiased estimator for KL values and gradients, with lower variance than sampled KL.

First, we consider Reverse KL. Let  $q_n$  be the set of top-k logit indices from  $\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)$  at sequence position n,

$$q_n \leftarrow \text{Topk}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)) \qquad q_n \subseteq \mathcal{V} \qquad |q_n| = k$$

we can rewrite reverse KL by partitioning the vocabulary:

$$\underbrace{\sum_{j \in q_n} \pi_{\theta}(j|h_n) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j|h_n)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j|h_n)}}_{\text{`Truncated' KL: } (\widehat{\text{KL}}_{\text{trun}})_n} + \underbrace{\sum_{j \not \in q_n} \pi_{\theta}(j|h_n) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j|h_n)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j|h_n)}}_{\text{`Tail' correction: } (\widehat{\text{KL}}_{\text{tail}})_n}$$

The second term can be estimated using sampled KL:

$$(\widehat{KL}_{tail})_n = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } y_n \in q_n \\ (\mathbf{K4})_n & \text{if } y_n \notin q_n \end{cases} \quad y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | h_n) \quad (25)$$

The **Top-k reverse KL** estimator is an addition of the two:

$$\overline{(\text{Topk}\mathbf{Reverse}KL)_n = (\widehat{KL}_{trun})_n + (\widehat{KL}_{tail})_n}$$
(26)

Similarly, we can estimate forward KL by splitting it into a truncated exact KL and a sampled tail term:

$$q_n \leftarrow \operatorname{Topk}(\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_n))$$

$$(\widehat{\operatorname{FKL}}_{\operatorname{trun}})_n = \sum_{j \in q_n} \pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(j \mid h_n) \log \frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)}$$

$$(\widehat{\operatorname{FKL}}_{\operatorname{tail}})_n = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } y_n \in q_n \\ (\mathbf{K5})_n & \text{if } y_n \notin q_n \end{cases} \quad y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)$$

The **Top-k forward KL** estimator is therefore:

$$\left| (\text{TopkForwardKL})_n = (\widehat{\text{FKL}}_{\text{trun}})_n + (\widehat{\text{FKL}}_{\text{tail}})_n \right| (27)$$

Top-k KL estimators are unbiased regardless of whether the chosen logit indices are top-k or not. Moreover, Top-k KL renders both exact KL and sampled KL as special cases. When  $k = |\mathcal{V}|$ , it is exact KL; when k = 0, it is sampled KL. Top-k KL allows for flexible interpolation between the two, remains unbiased at any k, and only requires O(k) memory. We generalize our estimator to any f-divergence in §H.

**Effect of EMA anchor** We empirically demonstrate the benefits of using an EMA target network instead of a fixed anchor for RL on LLMs. Note that using EMA does not require storing an additional set of weights during training, since the anchor (reference) policy weights already need to be stored for standard KL-regularized policy gradients.

First of all, we empirically find that keeping the KL with a small  $\beta > 0$  is necessary for preventing training collapse when doing full-finetuning (no LoRA). This is corroborated

by with the common practice of still using KL in large-scale RL training runs (Liu et al., 2025a; Kimi et al., 2025a).

Secondly, we find that in math reasoning, using an EMA anchor ( $\eta < 1$ ) improves GRPO across all evaluated datasets, as shown in Tab 3. For instance, using the EMA anchor gives us a boost of  $50.8\% \rightarrow 53.9\%$  on OlympiadBench. In addition, using an EMA anchor improves not just Pass@1 but Pass@16 as well, as shown in Fig 3; this suggests that target networks can effectively mitigate Pass@N collapse that is common in reasoning RL (Yue et al., 2025).

Using token-level KL instead of sequence-level KL is crucial for good performance. On agentic search, we compare against WARP (Ramé et al., 2024), which uses sequence-level KL (12) and EMA anchor; we find that WARP performs a lot worse than vanilla GRPO (10.6% vs 31.2%). When using token-level KL + EMA anchor, we see a gain from  $31.2\% \rightarrow 34.8\%$ . This validates our analysis in §5.1.

Effect of Top-k KL We find that the use of Top-k KL can make a substantial difference in RL performance; both Topk Forward KL (Alg 2) and Topk Reverse KL (Alg 1) simultaneously improve sample efficiency and asymptotic performance of RL. It is shown in Fig 5 that, compared to sampled KL, Top-k KL enables the learning process to rapidly take off and avoid premature convergence. This is because Top-k KL works like knowledge distillation, which uses logit-level information to provide dense supervision and lower gradient variance. In terms of the final performance (Tab 2), when used with EMA anchor, Top-k forward KL obtains a  $34.8\% \rightarrow 40.6\%$  gain compared to K3; Top-k reverse KL performs even better, achieving 41.6%.

**Other design choices** We now study two additional design choices in EMA-PG. (1) Firstly, since Fig 8 has found off-policy correction to be important especially for reverse

KL, we study the clipping range for the importance weight (IW), which is  $[s_{\min}, s_{\max}]$  in Alg 1 and 2. Prior literature has mixed views: (Guo et al., 2025) uses no off-policy correction ( $s_{\min} = s_{\max} = 1$ ); (Liu et al., 2025a) uses no clipping at all; (Zhang et al., 2025) uses aggressive clipping. Fig 9 finds that forward KL benefits from a tighter clipping range close to 1, but reverse KL benefits from more lenient clipping. This observation is consistent with the prevailing common practice that either uses forward KL (K3) without any off-policy correction or uses reverse KL with no clipping on IW. (2) Secondly, we also study whether the advantage normalization strategy inside the RL algorithm makes a difference in the presence of Top-k KL. The two choices are either group-wise normalization as done in GRPO or global advantage normalization (Hu et al., 2025) as done in the original PPO. We find that either choice did not make a noticeable difference to EMA-PG performance.

### 7. Conclusion

In this work, we introduced two simple changes to policy gradients on LLMs: EMA anchor, which changes what KL is regularizing against, and Top-k KL, which changes how KL gradients are computed. We showed that the two changes are highly effective. Future work includes further investigation into Top-k *f*-divergence estimators (§H.2) beyond just KL, the application of Top-k KL to both offline and on-policy knowledge distillation of LLMs, and the application of our sampled KL (and more broadly, *f*-divergence) estimators to the policy gradient operator itself (§J– §K).

The changes required to modify any policy gradient method into EMA-PG (with EMA anchor and Top-k KL) are highlighted above.

**Details of Top-k KL:** For completeness, we write down the full expression of Top-k KL terms. Using common notations:

$$y \sim \pi_{\text{old}}(\cdot \mid x)$$
  $h_n = (x, y_{0:n-1})$   $s_n = \operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_n \mid h_n)}\right)$   $s_n^{\text{clip}} = \operatorname{clip}(s_n, s_{\min}, s_{\max})$ 

The Top-k Reverse KL can be written as:

$$q_n \leftarrow \operatorname{Topk}(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid h_n)) \qquad (\widehat{\operatorname{KL}}_{\text{sampled}})_n = \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n))} \operatorname{sg}\left(\operatorname{log}\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}\right)$$

$$(\operatorname{Topk}\mathbf{Reverse}\operatorname{KL})_n = \sum_{j \in q_n} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \operatorname{log}\frac{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n)} + \mathbb{1}(y_n \not\in q_n) \cdot s_n^{\text{clip}} \cdot (\widehat{\operatorname{KL}}_{\text{sampled}})_n$$

And the Top-k Forward KL can be written as:

$$q_n \leftarrow \operatorname{Topk}(\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_n)) \qquad (\widehat{\operatorname{FKL}}_{\operatorname{sampled}})_n = \operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}\right) \operatorname{log}\frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)} + \operatorname{log}\frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n))}$$

$$(\operatorname{Topk}\mathbf{Forward}\operatorname{KL})_n = \sum_{j \in q_n} \pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(j \mid h_n) \operatorname{log}\frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)} + \mathbb{1}(y_n \not\in q_n) \cdot s_n^{\operatorname{clip}} \cdot (\widehat{\operatorname{FKL}}_{\operatorname{sampled}})_n$$

Then we sum up the token-wise KL along the sequence length:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{KL}} = \sum\nolimits_{n=0}^{L-1} (\mathrm{Topk} \mathbf{Reverse} \mathrm{KL})_n \qquad \widehat{\mathrm{FKL}} = \sum\nolimits_{n=0}^{L-1} (\mathrm{Topk} \mathbf{Forward} \mathrm{KL})_n$$

**Details of the Base RL Algorithm** We discuss some additional implementation details below.

• For the policy gradient loss function  $\mathcal{J}(\theta)$ , we use a standard version of GRPO: PPO-style clipping (Schulman et al., 2017b), Monte-Carlo estimation of advantages (Shao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a), with clip-higher (Yu et al., 2025):

$$\begin{split} y^{(i)} &\sim \log \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid x), \quad i = 0, \dots, N-1 \\ V(x) &= \frac{1}{N} \sum\nolimits_{i=0}^{N-1} R(x, y^{(i)}) \\ A(x, y^{(i)}) &= R(x, y^{(i)}) - V(x) \qquad \text{std}(x) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum\nolimits_{i=0}^{N-1} \left(R(x, y^{(i)}) - V(x)\right)^2} \\ s_n^{(i)} &= \text{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n^{(i)} \mid x, y_{0:n-1}^{(i)})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_n^{(i)} \mid x, y_{0:n-1}^{(i)})}\right) \qquad M_n^{(i)} &= \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } A(x, y^{(i)}) > 0 \text{ and } s_l^{(i)} > 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}}, \\ 0 & \text{if } A(x, y^{(i)}) < 0 \text{ and } s_l^{(i)} < 1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}}, \\ 1 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \\ \mathcal{J}^{\text{clip}}(x) &= \frac{1}{N} \frac{1}{L} \sum\nolimits_{i=0}^{N-1} \left(\sum\nolimits_{n=0}^{L-1} s_n^{(i)} M_n^{(i)} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_n^{(i)} \mid x, y_{0:n-1}^{(i)})\right) A(x, y^{(i)}) \\ \mathcal{J}(\theta) &= \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[\mathcal{J}^{\text{clip}}(x) / \text{std}(x)\right] \end{split}$$

where  $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$  and  $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$ .  $\mathcal{J}^{\text{clip}}$  applies the PPO-clip objective written in an equivalent form using binary masks (Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2025). Note that the two techniques of EMA-PG are orthogonal to how we compute  $\mathcal{J}(\theta)$ ; we can in principle also choose to use CISPO (Chen et al., 2025) or GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025) as the base algorithm.

- For the optimizer, we use Adam (Kingma, 2014) with decoupled weight decay (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), with a learning rate of 1e-6 for *full finetuning*. Since the focus of our study is to device a better algorithm for full-scale post-training, we do not use any low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) in our experiments.
- $\beta = 0.001$  for KL regularization.
- $T_{\rm ema}=10$ , following prior empirical practice among practitioners of Deep Q-learning that target networks (even though under EMA soft update) still only get updated every  $T_{\rm ema}$  iterations; (Zhang et al., 2021) set the value to 10 (see target\_update\_freq = 10 in their implementation).

# <span id="page-16-0"></span>C. Derivations for EMA Policy Gradient Dynamics

Fisher matrix The Fisher information matrix of a policy π<sup>θ</sup> is:

$$F_{\theta} \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ (\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)) (\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x))^{\top} \right]$$

The Fisher matrix is positive semidefinite (PSD), so all eigenvalues satisfy λ<sup>i</sup> ≥ 0. Consequently,

$$\lambda_{\max}(F_{\theta}) \le \operatorname{tr}(F_{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{\substack{x \sim \mathcal{D} \\ y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)}} \left[ \|\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)\|_{2}^{2} \right]$$
 (28)

giving us a practical way of estimating an upper bound on the largest Fisher eigenvalue of a policy under D.

Setup. Let J(θ) := R(πθ) denote the (expected) RL objective. Define the EMA parameters and the *lag*

$$\theta_{\mathrm{ema},t+1} = \eta \, \theta_{\mathrm{ema},t} + (1 - \eta) \, \theta_t, \qquad \eta \in [0,1),$$
  
$$\delta_t \coloneqq \theta_t - \theta_{\mathrm{ema},t}.$$

Let g<sup>t</sup> := ∇θJ(θ)|θ=θ<sup>t</sup> be the policy-gradient (or any ascent direction). We consider the update

<span id="page-16-4"></span>
$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \left[ g_t - \beta \nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta} \parallel \pi_{\theta_{\text{ema},t}}) \right]_{\theta = \theta_t}, \tag{29}$$

with step size α > 0 and regularization coefficient β ≥ 0.

# <span id="page-16-1"></span>C.1. Policy Gradient Dynamics under Local Quadratic KL Approximation

We assume a local quadratic approximation of the KL around θt:

$$KL(\pi_{\theta_t} \parallel \pi_{\theta_{\text{ema},t}}) \approx \frac{1}{2} (\theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema},t})^\top F_t(\theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema},t}) = \frac{1}{2} \delta_t^\top F_t \delta_t,$$
(30)

where F<sup>t</sup> := Fθ<sup>t</sup> is the (empirical) Fisher information matrix at θt. Differentiating the RHS of [\(30\)](#page-16-2) w.r.t. θ<sup>t</sup> yields

$$\nabla_{\theta_t} \operatorname{KL}(\pi_{\theta_t} \parallel \pi_{\theta_{\operatorname{ema},t}}) \approx \nabla_{\theta_t} \left( \frac{1}{2} \delta_t^{\top} F_t \delta_t \right) = F_t \delta_t, \tag{31}$$

where we treat F<sup>t</sup> as locally constant under this approximation. Substituting [\(31\)](#page-16-3) into [\(29\)](#page-16-4) gives

<span id="page-16-6"></span><span id="page-16-5"></span><span id="page-16-3"></span><span id="page-16-2"></span>
$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha g_t - \alpha \beta F_t \delta_t. \tag{32}$$

Lemma C.1 (Lag dynamics). *Under* [\(32\)](#page-16-5) *and the EMA recursion, the lag obeys*

$$\delta_{t+1} = (\eta I - \alpha \beta F_t) \, \delta_t + \alpha g_t. \tag{33}$$

*Proof.* By definition δt+1 = θt+1 − θema,t+1, and by the EMA update,

$$\theta_{\text{ema},t+1} = \eta \theta_{\text{ema},t} + (1 - \eta)\theta_t \implies \theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema},t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \theta_{\text{ema},t} - (1 - \eta)\theta_t = \eta(\theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema},t}) = \eta \delta_t. \tag{34}$$

Therefore,

$$\delta_{t+1} = (\theta_{t+1} - \theta_t) + (\theta_t - \theta_{\text{ema},t+1}) = (\theta_{t+1} - \theta_t) + \eta \, \delta_t. \tag{35}$$

Using [\(32\)](#page-16-5), θt+1 − θ<sup>t</sup> = αg<sup>t</sup> − αβFtδt, so

$$\delta_{t+1} = \eta \delta_t + \alpha g_t - \alpha \beta F_t \delta_t = (\eta I - \alpha \beta F_t) \delta_t + \alpha g_t. \tag{36}$$

Define the (time-varying) linear operator

$$D_t := \eta I - \alpha \beta F_t. \tag{37}$$

Collecting [\(32\)](#page-16-5) and [\(33\)](#page-16-6) gives the affine joint update

$$\begin{bmatrix} \theta_{t+1} \\ \delta_{t+1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I & -\alpha\beta F_t \\ 0 & \eta I - \alpha\beta F_t \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \theta_t \\ \delta_t \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \alpha I \\ \alpha I \end{bmatrix} g_t.$$
 (38)

### <span id="page-17-0"></span>C.2. Quasi-steady state analysis over a k-step window (Proof of Lemma 4.1)

**Assumption (frozen gradient and Fisher).** Fix an integer  $k \ge 1$  and assume that over the window  $\{t, \dots, t+k-1\}$ ,

$$g_{t+j} \approx g, \qquad F_{t+j} \approx F \qquad \text{for } j = 0, \dots, k-1,$$
 (39)

for some constant vectors/matrices g, F. Define the corresponding constant operator

$$D := \eta I - \alpha \beta F. \tag{40}$$

# C.2.1. CLOSED FORM FOR $\delta_{t+k}$

Unrolling (33) with constant D and g yields

$$\delta_{t+1} = D\delta_t + \alpha g,$$
  

$$\delta_{t+2} = D\delta_{t+1} + \alpha g = D^2 \delta_t + \alpha (D+I)g,$$
  

$$\vdots$$

$$\delta_{t+k} = D^k \delta_t + \alpha \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} D^j g. \tag{41}$$

Define the geometric-series matrix

<span id="page-17-2"></span><span id="page-17-1"></span>
$$S_k := \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} D^j. \tag{42}$$

Then (41) becomes

$$\delta_{t+k} = D^k \delta_t + \alpha S_k g. \tag{43}$$

# C.2.2. Invertibility of I-D and closed form for $S_k$

Let  $v_i$  be an eigenvector of F, and  $\lambda_i$  be its corresponding eigenvalue. Using  $D = \eta I - \alpha \beta F$ ,

$$Dv_i = (\eta I - \alpha \beta F)v_i = (\eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_i)v_i. \tag{44}$$

Define

$$\chi_i \coloneqq \eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_i. \tag{45}$$

Then  $Dv_i = \chi_i v_i$ . Since  $\lambda_i \geq 0$  ( $F \succeq 0$ ),  $\eta < 1$ , and  $\beta, \alpha \geq 0$ , we have

<span id="page-17-3"></span>
$$\chi_i \le \eta < 1,\tag{46}$$

Hence  $1 - \chi_i > 0$  for all i, so I - D is invertible. Consequently,

$$S_k = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} D^j = (I - D^k)(I - D)^{-1}.$$
 (47)

# C.2.3. Closed form for $\theta_{t+k}$ and the $M_k$ matrix

From (32) with frozen F, g,

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha g - \alpha \beta F \delta_t, \tag{48}$$

$$\theta_{t+k} = \theta_t + \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \left( \alpha g - \alpha \beta F \delta_{t+j} \right) = \theta_t + \alpha k g - \alpha \beta F \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \delta_{t+j}. \tag{49}$$

Using (43) at times t + j,

$$\delta_{t+j} = D^j \delta_t + \alpha S_j g, \tag{50}$$

so

$$\sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \delta_{t+j} = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} D^j \delta_t + \alpha \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} S_j g = S_k \delta_t + \alpha \left( \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} S_j \right) g.$$
 (51)

Define

<span id="page-18-1"></span>
$$M_k := \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} S_j.$$
 (52)

Then

$$\theta_{t+k} = \theta_t + \alpha k g - \alpha \beta F S_k \delta_t - \alpha^2 \beta F M_k g. \tag{53}$$

Closed form for  $M_k$ . Using  $S_j = (I - D^j)(I - D)^{-1}$  from (47),

$$M_k = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} (I - D^j)(I - D)^{-1} = \left(kI - \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} D^j\right)(I - D)^{-1} = (kI - S_k)(I - D)^{-1}.$$
 (54)

Collecting (53) and (43), one may the dynamics as a homogeneous linear map in  $(\theta, \delta, g)$ :

$$\begin{bmatrix} \theta_{t+k} \\ \delta_{t+k} \\ g \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I & -\alpha\beta F S_k & \alpha k I - \alpha^2 \beta F M_k \\ 0 & D^k & \alpha S_k \\ 0 & 0 & I \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \theta_t \\ \delta_t \\ g \end{bmatrix}$$

### <span id="page-18-0"></span>C.3. Eigen-decomposition of Fisher and mode-wise dynamics (Proof of Lemma 4.2)

Since F is a Fisher information matrix (or symmetric empirical approximation), we assume F is symmetric PSD:

$$F \succ 0$$
.

Therefore, there exists an orthonormal eigenbasis  $\{v_i\}_{i=0}^{d-1}$  and eigenvalues  $\lambda_i \geq 0$  such that

$$Fv_i = \lambda_i v_i, \quad \lambda_i > 0.$$

We refer to  $v_i$  as the *i*-th Fisher eigenvector (eigen-direction). "High curvature" corresponds to large  $\lambda_i$ . Previously we have already shown that

$$\chi_i := \eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_i \qquad \chi_i \le \eta < 1,$$

and  $\chi_i$  may be negative if  $\alpha\beta\lambda_i > \eta$ . Now we define the scalar projections

<span id="page-18-2"></span>
$$\delta_t^{(i)} \coloneqq v_i^\top \delta_t, \qquad g^{(i)} \coloneqq v_i^\top g. \tag{55}$$

Projecting (43) onto  $v_i$  yields

$$\delta_{t+k}^{(i)} = v_i^{\top} D^k \delta_t + \alpha v_i^{\top} S_k g = \chi_i^k \delta_t^{(i)} + \alpha \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \chi_i^j g^{(i)}$$
(56)

$$= \chi_i^k \delta_t^{(i)} + \alpha \frac{1 - \chi_i^k}{1 - \chi_i} g^{(i)}, \tag{57}$$

This yields the decomposition into a transient term and a particular solution.

### <span id="page-19-0"></span>C.4. Stability and oscillations

### C.4.1. STABILITY CONDITION

The transient part of the lag dynamics is  $\delta_{t+1} = D\delta_t$ . Stability (transient  $\to 0$ ) requires  $\rho(D) < 1$ , equivalently

<span id="page-19-2"></span>
$$\max_{i} |\chi_i| < 1. \tag{58}$$

Since  $\chi_i \leq \eta < 1$ , the upper inequality  $|\chi_i| < 1$  is only violated if  $\chi_i \leq -1$  for some i. The most negative eigenvalue occurs at  $\lambda_{\max} := \max_i \lambda_i$ , so stability is equivalent to

$$\eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} > -1 \iff \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} < 1 + \eta.$$
(59)

This proves (9).

### C.4.2. Non-oscillatory vs. oscillatory transients

From (57), the sign of the transient in mode i is governed by  $\chi_i^k$ . If  $\chi_i \in (0,1)$ , the transient decays monotonically. If  $\chi_i \in (-1,0)$ , the transient alternates sign each step (damped overshoot/undershoot), which we call "oscillatory". Thus:

• Non-oscillatory in all modes iff  $\chi_i \geq 0$  for all i, i.e.

$$\eta - \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} \ge 0 \iff \alpha \beta \lambda_{\text{max}} \le \eta.$$
(60)

• Oscillatory in some high-curvature directions if there exists i with  $\chi_i < 0$ , which can occur when  $\alpha \beta \lambda_{\max} > \eta$ .

Combined with stability (59) yields the three regimes summarized in the main text:

- If  $0 \le \alpha \beta \lambda_{\max} \le \eta$ , then  $\chi_i \in [0, \eta] \subset [0, 1)$  for all i, so the dynamics is stable and all transients decay monotonically (non-oscillatory).
- If  $\eta < \alpha \beta \lambda_{\max} < 1 + \eta$ , then stability holds ( $|\chi_i| < 1$  for all i) but there exist high-curvature directions with  $\chi_i < 0$ , producing damped sign-flipping transients (oscillations).
- If  $\alpha\beta\lambda_{\max} \geq 1 + \eta$ , then  $\chi_{\max} \leq -1$  for the top-curvature direction, so  $\max_i |\chi_i| \geq 1$  and the lag dynamics is unstable.

### <span id="page-19-1"></span>C.5. Steady-state lag and bounds

Assume stability (59),  $D^k \to 0$  as  $k \to \infty$ . Taking  $k \to \infty$  in (43) gives the steady lag

$$\delta_* = \lim_{k \to \infty} \delta_{t+k} = \alpha \left( \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} D^j \right) g = \alpha (I - D)^{-1} g.$$
(61)

Using  $I - D = I - (\eta I - \alpha \beta F) = (1 - \eta)I + \alpha \beta F$ , we obtain

$$\delta_* = \alpha \left( (1 - \eta)I + \alpha \beta F \right)^{-1} g,\tag{62}$$

In each eigen-direction,

<span id="page-19-3"></span>
$$\delta_*^{(i)} = \frac{\alpha}{(1-\eta) + \alpha\beta\lambda_i} g^{(i)}.$$
 (63)

### C.5.1. EUCLIDEAN NORM UPPER BOUND

Since  $F \succeq 0$ , we have the PSD ordering

$$(1-\eta)I + \alpha\beta F \succeq (1-\eta)I.$$

For positive definite matrices, PSD ordering reverses under inversion, hence

$$((1-\eta)I + \alpha\beta F)^{-1} \leq \frac{1}{1-\eta}I.$$

Therefore,

$$\|\delta_*\|_2 = \alpha \left\| \left( (1 - \eta)I + \alpha \beta F \right)^{-1} g \right\|_2 \le \alpha \left\| \left( (1 - \eta)I + \alpha \beta F \right)^{-1} \right\|_2 \|g\|_2 \le \alpha \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \eta} \|g\|_2, \tag{64}$$

### C.5.2. QUASI-STEADY KL MAGNITUDE

Under the quadratic KL approximation (30), the quasi-steady KL is

$$\widehat{KL}_* \approx \frac{1}{2} \delta_*^{\top} F \delta_*. \tag{65}$$

Expanding in the eigenbasis,  $F = \sum_i \lambda_i v_i v_i^{\top}$  and  $\delta_* = \sum_i \delta_*^{(i)} v_i$ , gives

$$\delta_*^{\top} F \delta_* = \sum_{i=0}^{d-1} \lambda_i \left( \delta_*^{(i)} \right)^2. \tag{66}$$

Substituting (63) yields

$$\widehat{KL}_* \approx \frac{\alpha^2}{2} \sum_{i=0}^{d-1} \frac{\lambda_i (g^{(i)})^2}{\left((1-\eta) + \alpha \beta \lambda_i\right)^2},\tag{67}$$

Thus, within the quasi-steady model, increasing  $\eta$  increases  $\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_*$  unless  $\beta$  is increased or  $\alpha$  is decreased.

# <span id="page-21-0"></span>D. Sequence-level KL vs. Token-level KL

**Notation and setup** For prompt x, consider the conditional distribution over output sequence  $y = (y_0, \dots, y_{L-1})$ .

$$\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) = \prod_{n=0}^{L-1} \pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid x, y_{0:n-1}).$$

For each position l, define the prefix (history)

$$h_n \triangleq (x, y_{0:n-1}),$$

and the per-token log-ratio and score term

$$\rho_n \triangleq \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n)}, \qquad \omega_n \triangleq \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n).$$

Note that  $\log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} = \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \rho_n$  and  $\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) = \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \omega_n$ .

### <span id="page-21-1"></span>D.1. Sequence-level KL gradients

Define the (sequence-level) conditional KL:

$$\mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \parallel \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid x)\big) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x)} \right].$$

Differentiate it:

$$\nabla_{\theta} KL = \nabla_{\theta} \int \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{ref}(y \mid x)} dy$$
$$= \int \nabla_{\theta} \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{ref}(y \mid x)} dy + \int \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) dy.$$

Using  $\nabla_{\theta}\pi_{\theta} = \pi_{\theta}\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}$  and  $\int \pi_{\theta}\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}\,dy = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}}[\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}] = 0$ , we obtain the standard score-function form:

$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{KL} \left( \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x) \right) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \right]. \tag{68}$$

### <span id="page-21-2"></span>D.2. Analysis of sequence-level KL gradients

Substitute the autoregressive decompositions:

$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{KL} \left( \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x) \right) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \left( \sum_{m=0}^{L-1} \rho_m \right) \left( \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \omega_n \right) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \sum_{m=0}^{L-1} \rho_m \omega_n \right]. \tag{69}$$

Split the inner sum into "future" and "past" relative to n:

<span id="page-21-4"></span>
$$\sum_{m=0}^{L-1} \rho_m \omega_n = \left(\sum_{m \ge n} \rho_m\right) \omega_n + \left(\sum_{m < n} \rho_m\right) \omega_n. \tag{70}$$

Why the "past" terms vanish Recall the conditional expectation notation

<span id="page-21-3"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_n[\cdot] \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{y_n \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | h_n)}[\cdot], \qquad h_n \triangleq (x, y_{0:n-1}).$$

We will use the standard Law of total expectation in the following explicit form: for any random variable  $Z = Z(x, y_{0:n})$ ,

$$\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)}[Z] = \mathbb{E}_{y_{0:n-1} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \Big[ \mathbb{E}_{y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x, y_{0:n-1})}[Z] \Big] = \mathbb{E}_{y_{0:n-1} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \Big[ \mathbb{E}_n[Z] \Big]. \tag{71}$$

Step 1: the score has zero mean. Conditioning on a fixed prefix hn, we have

$$\mathbb{E}_n[\omega_n] = \sum_a \pi_\theta(a \mid h_n) \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid h_n) = \sum_a \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(a \mid h_n) = \nabla_\theta \sum_a \pi_\theta(a \mid h_n) = \nabla_\theta 1 = 0.$$

Step 2: past log-ratios are constant under En[·]. Since P m<n ρ<sup>m</sup> depends only on the prefix h<sup>n</sup> = (x, y0:n−1), it does not depend on y<sup>n</sup> and can be pulled out of En[·]:

$$\mathbb{E}_n \left[ \left( \sum_{m < n} \rho_m \right) \omega_n \right] = \left( \sum_{m < n} \rho_m \right) \mathbb{E}_n[\omega_n] = 0.$$

Step 3: unconditional past terms also vanish. Apply [\(71\)](#page-21-3) with Z = P m<n ρ<sup>m</sup> ωn:

$$\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \left( \sum_{m < n} \rho_m \right) \omega_n \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y_{0:n-1} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \mathbb{E}_n \left[ \left( \sum_{m < n} \rho_m \right) \omega_n \right] \right] = 0, \tag{72}$$

which justifies dropping the "past tokens" contribution in the main derivation.

### <span id="page-22-0"></span>D.3. Token-level KL gradients

Define the token-level conditional KL at position n:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}_n \triangleq \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n) \, \| \, \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_n)\big) = \mathbb{E}_{y_n \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)} \left[ \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_n \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_n \mid h_n)} \right].$$

By the same argument as [\(68\)](#page-21-4), its gradient can be written as

$$\nabla_{\theta} \widehat{KL}_{n} = \mathbb{E}_{y_{n} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n})} \left[ \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_{n} \mid h_{n})}{\pi_{ref}(y_{n} \mid h_{n})} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_{n} \mid h_{n}) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y_{n} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n})} [\rho_{n} \omega_{n}].$$
 (73)

Hence the token-level KL objective yields the per-token weighting:

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\text{kl}}^{\text{token}} = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \nabla_{\theta} \widehat{\text{KL}}_n \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} \rho_n \omega_n \right]. \tag{74}$$

Comparison Sequence-level KL weights token n by the cumulative *future* log-ratio P m≥n ρm, while token-level KL weights token n only by the *local* log-ratio ρn.

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{seq}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} \Big[ \sum_{n} \Big( \sum_{\substack{m \geq n \\ m \geq n}} \rho_{m} \Big) \, \omega_{n} \Big] \qquad \nabla_{\theta} L_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{token}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} \Big[ \sum_{n} \Big( \rho_{n} \Big) \, \omega_{n} \Big]$$

In other words, sequence-level KL punishes each current token for the divergence of all its future tokens; token-level KL is only concerned with the current token.

# <span id="page-23-0"></span>E. Derivation of Sampled Reverse KL Estimator

We focus on a fixed token position n with prefix h<sup>n</sup> = (x, y0:n−1). Let p ∼ πθ(· | hn) be the sampled token, and define

$$w(p) \triangleq \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(p \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n)}, \qquad \ell(p) \triangleq -\log w(p) = \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(p \mid h_n)}.$$

To avoid ambiguity, throughout this subsection we use the shorthand

<span id="page-23-3"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_p[\,\cdot\,] \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | h_n)}[\,\cdot\,]$$

Reverse KL at a fixed prefix. The token-level reverse KL at history h<sup>n</sup> is (with V being the vocabulary):

$$\mathrm{KL}_{n}(\theta) \triangleq \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n}) \parallel \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_{n})\big) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n}) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n})}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j \mid h_{n})} = \mathbb{E}_{p}\big[\ell(p)\big].$$

Exact reverse-KL gradient.

$$\nabla_{\theta} \mathrm{KL}_{n}(\theta) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j)} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j) = \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n})} \Big[ \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) \Big].$$

Ep[∇θ(·)] versus ∇θEp[·] Since πθ(· | hn) depends on θ, Ep∼πθ(·|hn) [·] is itself a function of θ; we write it as Ep[·]. For any scalar function f(p, θ), the gradient ∇θEp[·] is:

$$\mathbb{E}_{p}\big[f(p,\theta)\big] = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n}) f(j,\theta) \qquad \nabla_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{p}\big[f(p,\theta)\big] = \mathbb{E}_{p}\Big[f(p,\theta) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) + \nabla_{\theta} f(p,\theta)\Big]. \tag{75}$$

In contrast, Ep[∇θf(p, θ)] alone is in general is not equal to ∇θEp[f], as it has only one of the two terms in the full gradient.

# <span id="page-23-1"></span>E.1. Sampled KL estimators (K1 / K2 / K3) and their expected values

Define the single-sample estimators

$$(K1)_n \triangleq \ell(p), \qquad (K2)_n \triangleq \frac{1}{2} \ell(p)^2, \qquad (K3)_n \triangleq \ell(p) + w(p) - 1.$$
 (76)

Then

$$\mathbb{E}_p[(\mathrm{K1})_n] = \mathbb{E}_p[\ell(p)] = \mathrm{KL}_n(\theta), \tag{77}$$

$$\mathbb{E}_p[w(p)] = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n) = 1, \tag{78}$$

$$\mathbb{E}_p\big[(\mathrm{K3})_n\big] = \mathbb{E}_p\big[\ell(p)\big] + \mathbb{E}_p\big[w(p)\big] - 1 = \mathrm{KL}_n(\theta),\tag{79}$$

while in general E<sup>p</sup> -(K2)<sup>n</sup> = 1 2 Ep[ℓ(p) 2 ] ̸= Ep[ℓ(p)], so K2 has incorrect expected values.

# <span id="page-23-2"></span>E.2. Gradients of K1 / K2 / K3 under autodiff Ep[∇θ(·)]

Here ∇θ(·) denotes the autodiff gradient of the sampled scalar while treating the sampled token p as fixed.

K1: correct value but wrong gradient. Since (K1)<sup>n</sup> = ℓ(p) = log πθ(p | hn) − log πref(p | hn) and πref is fixed,

<span id="page-23-4"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta}(\mathbf{K}1)_n = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n).$$

Averaging over p ∼ πθ(· | hn) gives

$$\mathbb{E}_p \big[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathrm{K1})_n \big] = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \nabla_{\theta} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) = \nabla_{\theta} 1 = 0,$$

which is zero.

K2: correct reverse-KL gradient but wrong value. We have (K2)<sup>n</sup> = 1 2 ℓ(p) 2 , so

$$\nabla_{\theta}(K2)_n = \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \ell(p) = \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n).$$

Therefore,

$$\mathbb{E}_p \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (K2)_n \right] = \mathbb{E}_p \left[ \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta} (p \mid h_n) \right]. \tag{80}$$

K3: yields the forward-KL gradient under Ep[∇θ(·)]. Since (K3)<sup>n</sup> = ℓ(p) + w(p) − 1 and,

$$w(p) = \pi_{\text{ref}}(p \mid h_n) / \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n) \quad \nabla_{\theta} w(p) = -w(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n),$$
$$\nabla_{\theta}(K3)_n = \nabla_{\theta} \ell(p) + \nabla_{\theta} w(p) = (1 - w(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n).$$

Averaging over p ∼ πθ(· | hn) gives

$$\mathbb{E}_{p} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathrm{K3})_{n} \right] = \mathbb{E}_{p} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) \right] - \mathbb{E}_{p} \left[ w(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) \right] \\
= 0 - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n}) \frac{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j \mid h_{n})}{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n})} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n}) \\
= - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j \mid h_{n}) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_{n}) = \nabla_{\theta} \mathrm{KL} \left( \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_{n}) \parallel \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n}) \right), \tag{81}$$

which is the forward-KL gradient, not the reverse-KL gradient.

# <span id="page-24-0"></span>E.3. The r-trick restores ∇θEp[·] from Ep[∇θ(·)].

Define r(·) as:

<span id="page-24-2"></span>
$$r(p,\theta) \triangleq \frac{\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n)}{\operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n))}.$$
(82)

In the forward pass r(p, θ) = 1, but in backpropagation the denominator is treated as constant, hence

$$\nabla_{\theta} r(p, \theta) = r(p, \theta) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n) = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n).$$

For any scalar f(p, θ), differentiating r(p, θ)f(p, θ) gives

$$\nabla_{\theta}(rf) = f \nabla_{\theta} r + r \nabla_{\theta} f = f(p, \theta) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n) + \nabla_{\theta} f(p, \theta),$$

where we used r = 1 in the forward pass so r ∇θf = ∇θf. Taking Ep[·] on both sides and comparing with [\(75\)](#page-23-3) yields

$$\mathbb{E}_{p} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (r(p, \theta) f(p, \theta)) \right] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{p} \left[ f(p, \theta) \right]. \tag{83}$$

This identity holds even when f depends on θ (via the ∇θf term).

# <span id="page-24-1"></span>E.4. K3++ yields unbiased reverse-KL values and gradients

Define

$$(K3^{++})_n \triangleq r(p,\theta) (K3)_n = r(p,\theta) (\ell(p) + w(p) - 1).$$
 (84)

*Value:* since r = 1 in the forward pass,

$$\mathbb{E}_p[(\mathrm{K3}^{++})_n] = \mathbb{E}_p[(\mathrm{K3})_n] = \mathrm{KL}_n(\theta) \quad \text{by (79)}. \tag{85}$$

*Gradient:* by [\(83\)](#page-24-2) with f = (K3)n,

$$\mathbb{E}_p \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathbf{K3}^{++})_n \right] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_p \left[ (\mathbf{K3})_n \right] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbf{KL}_n(\theta), \tag{86}$$

because Ep[(K3)n] = KLn(θ).

### E.4.1. ALTERNATIVE PROOF

Alternatively, we compute the K3++ gradients in a brute force manner by directly differentiating using the product rule:

<span id="page-25-3"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta}(K3^{++})_{n} = (\ell + w - 1) \nabla_{\theta} r + r(\nabla_{\theta} \ell + \nabla_{\theta} w). \tag{87}$$

Now compute each piece (all conditioned on the sampled p and fixed hn):

<span id="page-25-1"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta} r = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n), \tag{88}$$

<span id="page-25-2"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta} \ell = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n), \tag{89}$$

<span id="page-25-4"></span>
$$\nabla_{\theta} w = \nabla_{\theta} \left( \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(p \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n)} \right) = -w \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n). \tag{90}$$

Substitute [\(88\)](#page-25-1)–[\(90\)](#page-25-2) into [\(87\)](#page-25-3). Using that r = 1 in the forward pass,

$$\nabla_{\theta}(\mathrm{K3}^{++})_{n} = (\ell + w - 1) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) + (1 - w) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}) = \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n})$$
(91)

Taking expectation over the sampled token p ∼ πθ(· | hn) on both sides of [\(91\)](#page-25-4) yields

$$\mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathrm{K3}^{++})_n \right] = \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)} \left[ \ell(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n) \right] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathrm{KL}_n(\theta),$$

### <span id="page-25-0"></span>E.5. K4 yields unbiased reverse-KL values and gradients

Define

$$(K4)_n \triangleq r(p,\theta) \cdot sg((K1)_n) = r(p,\theta) \cdot sg(\ell(p)). \tag{92}$$

*Value:* since r = 1 in the forward pass,

$$\mathbb{E}_p[(\mathrm{K4})_n] = \mathbb{E}_p[\ell(p)] = \mathrm{KL}_n(\theta). \tag{93}$$

*Gradient:* sg(ℓ(p)) is treated as constant in backprop, so differentiating yields

$$\nabla_{\theta}(K4)_{n} = \operatorname{sg}(\ell(p)) \nabla_{\theta} r(p, \theta) = \operatorname{sg}(\ell(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_{n}). \tag{94}$$

Taking expectation over p ∼ πθ(· | hn) gives

$$\mathbb{E}_p \big[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathbf{K4})_n \big] = \mathbb{E}_p \Big[ \ell(p) \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta} (p \mid h_n) \Big] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbf{KL}_n(\theta), \tag{95}$$

# <span id="page-26-0"></span>F. Derivation of Sampled Forward KL Estimator

Similar to before, we focus on a fixed token position n with prefix h<sup>n</sup> = (x, y0:n−1). For brevity, write

$$\pi_{\theta}(j) \triangleq \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n), \qquad \pi_{\text{ref}}(j) \triangleq \pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n).$$

At this position we sample a token

$$p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n),$$

and define the importance ratio and stop-gradient ratio

$$w(p) \triangleq \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(p)}{\pi_{\theta}(p)}, \qquad r(p, \theta) \triangleq \frac{\pi_{\theta}(p)}{\text{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(p))}.$$

The expectation over p is also same as before:

<span id="page-26-6"></span><span id="page-26-3"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_p[\,\cdot\,] \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|h_n)}[\,\cdot\,]$$

### <span id="page-26-1"></span>F.1. Forward KL and its gradient

The token-wise forward KL is

$$\mathrm{FKL}_{n}(\theta) \triangleq \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot \mid h_{n}) \, \| \, \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_{n})\big) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j) \log \frac{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j)}{\pi_{\theta}(j)}.$$

Differentiating w.r.t. θ (note πref does not depend on θ),

$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{FKL}_{n}(\theta) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\text{ref}}(j) \, \nabla_{\theta} (\log \pi_{\text{ref}}(j) - \log \pi_{\theta}(j)) = -\sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\text{ref}}(j) \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j). \tag{96}$$

We can rewrite [\(96\)](#page-26-3) as an expectation over p ∼ πθ(· | hn) using w(p):

$$-\sum_{j\in\mathcal{V}} \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j) = -\sum_{j\in\mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j) \frac{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j)}{\pi_{\theta}(j)} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(j) = -\mathbb{E}_{p} \Big[ w(p) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p) \Big]. \tag{97}$$

Finally, we use the score-mean identity Ep[∇<sup>θ</sup> log πθ(p)] = 0 to obtain an equivalent form:

$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{FKL}_{n}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{p} \Big[ w(p) \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p) \Big] = \mathbb{E}_{p} \Big[ (1 - w(p)) \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p) \Big]. \tag{98}$$

# <span id="page-26-2"></span>F.2. The K5 estimator has unbiased value

We propose the forward-KL estimator

<span id="page-26-5"></span><span id="page-26-4"></span>
$$(\mathbf{K5})_n \triangleq \operatorname{sg}(w(p)) \log w(p) + \log r(p, \theta). \tag{99}$$

For the KL value estimation, note that r(p, θ) = 1 in the forward pass, so log r(p, θ) = 0 in value. Therefore

$$\mathbb{E}_{p}[(\mathbf{K5})_{n}] = \mathbb{E}_{p}[w(p)\log w(p)] = \sum_{j\in\mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j) \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j)}{\pi_{\theta}(j)} \log \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j)}{\pi_{\theta}(j)}$$
$$= \sum_{j\in\mathcal{V}} \pi_{\text{ref}}(j) \log \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j)}{\pi_{\theta}(j)} = \text{FKL}_{n}(\theta), \tag{100}$$

which is the token-wise forward KL.

### <span id="page-27-0"></span>F.3. The K5 estimator has unbiased gradient

We next show that Ep[∇θ(K5)n] = ∇θFKLn(θ).

First, compute the gradient of each term in [\(99\)](#page-26-4). Since sg(w(p)) is treated as a constant in backprop,

$$\nabla_{\theta} (\operatorname{sg}(w(p)) \log w(p)) = \operatorname{sg}(w(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log w(p).$$

Because log w(p) = log πref(p) − log πθ(p) and πref is fixed,

$$\nabla_{\theta} \log w(p) = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p)$$
  $\nabla_{\theta} \log r(p, \theta) = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p).$ 

Combining them yields the per-sample gradient

$$\nabla_{\theta}(\mathbf{K5})_n = -\operatorname{sg}(w(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p) + \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p) = (1 - w(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p). \tag{101}$$

Taking expectation over p ∼ πθ(· | hn) gives

$$\mathbb{E}_p[\nabla_{\theta}(\mathbf{K5})_n] = \mathbb{E}_p[(1 - w(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p)] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathrm{FKL}_n(\theta), \tag{102}$$

where the last equality follows from [\(98\)](#page-26-5).

### <span id="page-27-1"></span>F.4. Variance reduction in K5 gradients

We start from the exact forward-KL gradient written in the importance-weighted form (Eq. [\(97\)](#page-26-6)):

$$\nabla_{\theta} \text{FKL}_n(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)} \Big[ w(p) \, \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n) \Big], \qquad w(p) = \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(p \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(p \mid h_n)}.$$

A key observation is that under the same sampling distribution p ∼ πθ(· | hn), the weight w(p) has mean 1:

$$\mathbb{E}_{p \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n)}[w(p)] = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\text{ref}}(j \mid h_n) = 1.$$

Therefore, we can subtract this constant baseline from w(p) without changing the expectation of the gradient estimator. Indeed, using the score-mean identity Ep∼πθ(·|hn) [∇<sup>θ</sup> log πθ(p | hn)] = 0, we have

$$-\mathbb{E}_{p}\Big[w(p)\,\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}(p\mid h_{n})\Big] = -\mathbb{E}_{p}\Big[(w(p)-1)\,\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}(p\mid h_{n})\Big] - \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{p}\Big[1\cdot\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}(p\mid h_{n})\Big]}_{=0}$$
$$= \mathbb{E}_{p}\Big[(1-w(p))\,\nabla_{\theta}\log\pi_{\theta}(p\mid h_{n})\Big].$$

Thus, replacing −w(p) with (1 − w(p)) centered around 0 preserves an unbiased gradient while typically reducing variance, known as the use of a *baseline* in policy gradient methods.

# <span id="page-28-0"></span>G. Bias-Variance Tradeoff in Top-k KL: An Illustrative Synthetic Setup

This appendix specifies the synthetic setting used to produce Fig. [4.](#page-4-6) The goal is to isolate the *bias–variance* behavior of the Top-k Reverse KL estimator in a controlled regime that reflects how KL penalties are applied to LLM policies, while removing confounders such as long-horizon autoregressive dependencies.

### G.1. Token-wise i.i.d. simplification

Let πθ(· | hn) be an LLM policy over the vocabulary V at token position n given history h<sup>n</sup> = (x, y<n), and let πref(· | hn) be the reference policy. The token-level reverse KL is:

<span id="page-28-1"></span>
$$KL(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid h_n) \parallel \pi_{ref}(\cdot \mid h_n)) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n)}{\pi_{ref}(j \mid h_n)}.$$
(103)

To focus purely on the estimator (rather than the dynamics that produce hn), we adopt the following simplification:

(i.i.d. token-position assumption) Each token position contributes an i.i.d. draw from a common distribution over (π, πref), and we analyze a representative position whose ground-truth objective is the reverse KL in [\(103\)](#page-28-1). In other words, we ignore autoregressive coupling across positions when constructing synthetic tasks.

### G.2. Synthetic task and metric

Under this assumption, each token position is treated as a draw of a single-step categorical policy pair (π, πref) over a fixed vocabulary V. We fix |V| = 32000 and sample

$$\pi_{\text{ref}} = \operatorname{softmax}(\tilde{z}_{\text{ref}}), \qquad \tilde{z}_{\text{ref}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_V),$$
(104)

$$\pi = \operatorname{softmax}(s \cdot \tilde{z}), \qquad \tilde{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_V), \ s > 0.$$
 (105)

Controlling concentration via top-32 mass. Define the top-32 probability mass

$$M_{32}(\pi) \triangleq \sum_{j \in \text{TopK}(\pi,32)} \pi(j). \tag{106}$$

We tune the logit scale s so that M32(π) matches one of the target values

$$M_{32}(\pi) \approx m, \qquad m \in \{0.2, 0.5, 0.8, 0.9\},$$
 (107)

thereby probing a range of entropy regimes from diffuse to highly concentrated policies.

Estimators and error metric. We compare three per-token gradient estimators for the reverse-KL penalty: (i) *sampledonly* reverse KL (fully Monte Carlo), (ii) *truncated-only* (exact top-k head without tail correction; biased), and (iii) *Top-k* reverse KL (exact head plus masked sampled tail; unbiased). We sweep

$$K \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256\}, \qquad B \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192, 16384, 32768\},$$

where B denotes the number of i.i.d. samples drawn from π ("batch tokens") used by the sampled components of the estimators. For each estimator, we measure the relative root-mean-squared error (RMSE) of the per-token gradient:

<span id="page-28-2"></span>
$$RelRMSE \triangleq \frac{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\|\hat{g} - g_{true}\|_2^2]}}{\|g_{true}\|_2},$$
(108)

where gtrue is the exact gradient of [\(103\)](#page-28-1) with respect to the token logits at that position, and gˆ is the corresponding estimator. The expectation in [\(108\)](#page-28-2) is approximated by Monte Carlo sampling and averaging over many independently generated synthetic tasks.

Critical sample size. For each K, the truncated-only estimator induces a bias floor (its error does not vanish with more samples since it omits the tail). The Top-k estimator is unbiased but has sampling variance from the tail correction. We define the *critical sample size* as the smallest B beyond which Top-k KL achieves lower gradient error than Truncated KL, i.e., the intersection point between the Top-k RMSE curve and the truncated-only bias floor.

# <span id="page-29-0"></span>H. Top-k f-Divergence Estimator

We generalize the Top-k "truncated exact term + masked sampled tail" construction from KL to a broad class of fdivergences, while retaining (i) unbiased value estimates and (ii) unbiased gradients under the convention E[∇θ(·)] (i.e., the expectation of autodiff gradients of sampled scalars).

Setup and notation. Fix a token position n and prefix h<sup>n</sup> = (x, y0:n−1). Let the current policy at this position be

$$P_{\theta}(j) \triangleq \pi_{\theta}(j \mid h_n), \quad j \in \mathcal{V},$$

and let the reference policy be

$$Q(j) \triangleq \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(j \mid h_n),$$

which is treated as independent of θ. We use an explicit expectation shorthand:

<span id="page-29-2"></span>
$$\mathbb{E}_p[\,\cdot\,] \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{p \sim P_\theta(\cdot)}[\,\cdot\,]$$

### <span id="page-29-1"></span>H.1. f-divergence and its gradients

Let f : R<sup>+</sup> → R be convex with f(1) = 0. The (discrete) f-divergence is

$$D_f(P_\theta || Q) \triangleq \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} Q(j) f\left(\frac{P_\theta(j)}{Q(j)}\right). \tag{109}$$

Define the likelihood ratio and its inverse

$$t(j) \triangleq \frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}, \qquad w(j) \triangleq \frac{Q(j)}{P_{\theta}(j)} = \frac{1}{t(j)}.$$
 (110)

Eq. [\(109\)](#page-29-2) can be written as an expectation under Q:

$$D_f(P_\theta || Q) = \mathbb{E}_{j \sim Q} [f(t(j))]. \tag{111}$$

It also admits an equivalent importance-weighted expectation under Pθ. Define

<span id="page-29-4"></span><span id="page-29-3"></span>
$$g_f(w) \triangleq w f(1/w). \tag{112}$$

Then

$$D_{f}(P_{\theta}||Q) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} Q(j) f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}\right) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} P_{\theta}(j) \frac{Q(j)}{P_{\theta}(j)} f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}\right)$$
$$= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} P_{\theta}(j) g_{f}(w(j)) = \mathbb{E}_{p} \left[g_{f}(w(p))\right], \tag{113}$$

where p ∼ Pθ(·) and w(p) = Q(p)/Pθ(p).

# H.1.1. EXACT GRADIENT W.R.T. θ (REFERENCE POLICY FIXED)

Since Q is fixed, differentiating [\(109\)](#page-29-2) yields

$$\nabla_{\theta} D_{f}(P_{\theta} \| Q) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} Q(j) f'(t(j)) \nabla_{\theta} \left( \frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)} \right) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} f'(t(j)) \nabla_{\theta} P_{\theta}(j)$$

$$= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} P_{\theta}(j) f'(t(j)) \nabla_{\theta} \log P_{\theta}(j) = \mathbb{E}_{p} \Big[ f'(t(p)) \nabla_{\theta} \log P_{\theta}(p) \Big]. \tag{114}$$

Eq. [\(114\)](#page-29-3) is the policy-gradient-shaped form used throughout this appendix.

# <span id="page-30-0"></span>H.2. A generic, unbiased, sampled f-divergence estimator

We define the following quantities as before:

$$r(p) \triangleq \frac{P_{\theta}(p)}{\operatorname{sg}(P_{\theta}(p))}$$
  $g_f(w(p)) \triangleq w(p) f(1/w(p))$   $w(p) = \frac{Q(p)}{P_{\theta}(p)}$ 

Then we have our sampled f-divergence estimator, Kf ++:

<span id="page-30-4"></span><span id="page-30-2"></span>
$$(\mathbf{K}f^{++})_n \triangleq r(p) \, g_f(w(p)), \qquad p \sim P_\theta(\cdot). \tag{115}$$

Which in effect becomes:

$$(\mathbf{K}f^{++})_n = \operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{Q(p)}{P_{\theta}(p)}\right) f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(p)}{Q(p)}\right) = \operatorname{sg}(w) f(1/w)$$
(116)

Another way to write this expression is (Kf ++)<sup>n</sup> = sg πref(p|hn) πθ(p|hn) f πθ(p|hn) πref(p|hn) .

Unbiased value. Since r = 1 in the forward pass, from [\(113\)](#page-29-4) we have

$$\mathbb{E}_p[(\mathbf{K}f^{++})_n] = \mathbb{E}_p[g_f(w(p))] = D_f(P_\theta \| Q). \tag{117}$$

Unbiased gradient under E[∇θ(·)]. Differentiate [\(115\)](#page-30-2) using product rule:

$$\nabla_{\theta}(\mathbf{K}f^{++})_n = g_f(w(p)) \nabla_{\theta}r(p) + r(p) \nabla_{\theta}g_f(w(p)) = g_f(w(p)) \nabla_{\theta}\log P_{\theta}(p) + \nabla_{\theta}g_f(w(p))$$
(118)

Taking expectation over p ∼ Pθ(·) gives

$$\mathbb{E}_p \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (\mathbf{K} f^{++})_n \right] = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_p \left[ g_f(w(p)) \right] = \nabla_{\theta} D_f(P_{\theta} \| Q), \tag{119}$$

where the last equality follows from [\(113\)](#page-29-4). Thus (Kf ++)<sup>n</sup> provides both unbiased values and unbiased gradients for any f-divergence.

### <span id="page-30-1"></span>H.3. Top-k f-divergence estimator: A generalization of Top-k KL estimator

Let S ⊆ V be any index set of size K (e.g., S = Topk(Pθ) or S = Topk(Q)). Being an unbiased estimator does not require S to be Top-K; Top-K is a variance/compute heuristic.

Truncated exact term. Define the truncated f-divergence (computed exactly given logits on S):

<span id="page-30-3"></span>
$$\left| (\widehat{D}_{f,\text{trun}})_n \triangleq \sum_{j \in S} Q(j) f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}\right) \right|$$
(120)

Masked sampled tail term. Sample p ∼ Pθ(·) and define the masked tail estimator

$$\widehat{(\widehat{D}_{f,\text{tail}})_n} \triangleq \mathbf{1}\{p \notin S\} (\mathbf{K}f^{++})_n = \mathbf{1}\{p \notin S\} r(p) g_f \left(\frac{Q(p)}{P_{\theta}(p)}\right) \tag{121}$$

Top-k f-divergence estimator. We define the Top-K f-divergence estimator

$$(\text{TopK-}D_f)_n \triangleq (\widehat{D}_{f,\text{trun}})_n + (\widehat{D}_{f,\text{tail}})_n$$
 (122)

Unbiased value. Using [\(113\)](#page-29-4) and the definition of g<sup>f</sup> ,

$$\mathbb{E}_{p}\left[(\widehat{D}_{f,\text{tail}})_{n}\right] = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} P_{\theta}(j) \mathbf{1}\{j \notin S\} g_{f}\left(\frac{Q(j)}{P_{\theta}(j)}\right) = \sum_{j \notin S} Q(j) f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}\right), \tag{123}$$

so

$$\mathbb{E}_p\big[(\text{TopK-}D_f)_n\big] = (\widehat{D}_{f,\text{trun}})_n + \mathbb{E}_p\big[(\widehat{D}_{f,\text{tail}})_n\big] = D_f(P_\theta \| Q). \tag{124}$$

**Unbiased gradient.** Since  $(\widehat{D}_{f,\text{trun}})_n$  is computed exactly on S, it has the correct gradient contribution from indices in S. For the tail term,

$$\mathbb{E}_{p}\left[\nabla_{\theta}(\widehat{D}_{f,\text{tail}})_{n}\right] = \mathbb{E}_{p}\left[\mathbf{1}\left\{p \notin S\right\} \nabla_{\theta}(\mathbf{K}f^{++})_{n}\right] = \nabla_{\theta} \sum_{j \notin S} Q(j) f\left(\frac{P_{\theta}(j)}{Q(j)}\right), \tag{125}$$

where the last step follows by expanding the expectation as a sum over  $j \notin S$  and using the property (119) of our sampled f-divergence estimator  $\mathbf{K}f^{++}$  restricted to the tail indices. Therefore,

$$\mathbb{E}_p \left[ \nabla_{\theta} (\text{TopK-}D_f)_n \right] = \nabla_{\theta} D_f (P_{\theta} \| Q). \tag{126}$$

# A note on variance reduction in Forward and Reverse KL gradient estimators.

- The Forward KL estimator derived from the standard f-divergence formula in Eq (116) is  $sg(w) \log w$ , which differs from our K5 estimator  $sg(w) \log w + \log r$ . This is explained in §F.4: the additional term  $\log r$  is added as a *baseline* to reduce gradient variance, where the baseline is  $\mathbb{E}[sg(w)] = 1$ .
- The Reverse KL estimator derived from Eq (116) is  $r \cdot (-\log w)$ ; this also differs from our K4 estimator  $r \cdot \operatorname{sg}(-\log w)$ . The reason is similar: for reverse KL, the baseline is  $\mathbb{E}[-\log w] = 0$ , and  $\mathbb{E}[-\nabla_{\theta} \log w] = \mathbb{E}[\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)] = 0$ . As a result, doing a stop gradient operation on  $(-\log w)$  keeps the gradients unbiased while reducing variance, so we do a stop gradient on the  $(-\log w)$  term in K4.

# <span id="page-32-0"></span>I. Optimal Policies under Forward vs Reverse KL Regularization

We now study the optimal policies under different types of regularizers and see if the optimal policies are interchangeable. The RL objectives under reverse and forward KL regularization are:

<span id="page-32-3"></span><span id="page-32-2"></span>
$$J = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)}[R(x,y)] - \beta \operatorname{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x) \| \pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(\cdot|x))$$
(127)

$$J_f = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)}[R(x,y)] - \beta \text{KL}(\pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x) || \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x))$$
(128)

**Optimal Policy under Reverse KL** It is well known that the optimal policy for objective (127) is (Peters et al., 2010; Schulman et al., 2017a; Abdolmaleki et al., 2018):

$$\pi^*(y|x) = \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \frac{\exp(R(x,y)/\beta)}{Z(x)} \qquad Z(x) = \sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp(R(x,y)/\beta)$$
(129)

**Optimal Policy under Forward KL** We derive the optimal policy for objective (128). Expanding the forward KL:

$$J_f = \sum_{y} \pi_{\theta}(y|x) R(x,y) + \beta \sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) + \text{const}$$
(130)

where the constant absorbs terms independent of  $\pi_{\theta}$ . This objective decomposes into an RL term (first sum) and a behavioral cloning (BC) term (second sum). Forming the Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{y} \pi_{\theta}(y|x) R(x,y) + \beta \sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \lambda \left( \sum_{y} \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - 1 \right)$$

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \pi_{\theta}(y|x)} = R(x,y) + \beta \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} - \lambda = 0$$

$$\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) = \frac{\beta \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)}{\lambda - R(x,y)}$$

For this to be a valid probability distribution, we need  $\lambda > \max_y R(x,y)$ . Denote  $Z^*(x) := \lambda$ , we require  $Z^*(x)$  to satisfy:

<span id="page-32-4"></span>
$$\sum_{y} \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)}{Z^{*}(x) - R(x, y)} = \frac{1}{\beta}$$
 (131)

Equation (131) implicitly defines  $Z^*(x)$  as a function of the reward distribution as well. The optimal policy is:

$$\pi_f^*(y \mid x) = \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \frac{\beta}{Z^*(x) - R(x, y)}$$
(132)

A similar result was obtained in (Grill et al., 2020a) under a different context.

### <span id="page-32-1"></span>I.1. Reward transformation for equivalent optima between forward KL and reverse KL

We now derive the reward transformation that makes the two objectives yield the same optimal policy.

$$\pi^*(y \mid x) = \pi_f^*(y \mid x) \qquad \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp(\tilde{R}/\beta)}{Z(x)} = \frac{\beta \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)}{Z^*(x) - R(x, y)}$$
(133)

Therefore, the optimal policy under reward function R and forward KL regularizer is equivalent to the counterpart under reward function  $\tilde{R}$  and reverse KL regularizer:

Forward KL (with 
$$R$$
)  $\rightarrow$  Reverse KL (with  $\tilde{R}$ ) 
$$\tilde{R} = -\beta \log(Z^* - R) + \beta \log(\beta Z)$$
 (134)

**Proposition I.1.** The function  $\tilde{R} = -\beta \log(Z^* - R) + const$  is strictly monotonically increasing and strictly convex in R.

*Proof.* Taking the derivative with respect to R (treating  $Z^*$  as constant):  $\frac{\partial \tilde{R}}{\partial R} = -\beta \cdot \frac{-1}{Z^* - R} = \frac{\beta}{Z^* - R} > 0$ . The last step was due to  $Z^* > \max_y R(x,y)$  by construction. Thus  $\tilde{R}$  is strictly monotonically increasing in R.

Taking the second derivative: 
$$\frac{\partial^2 \tilde{R}}{\partial R^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial R} \left( \frac{\beta}{Z^* - R} \right) = \beta \cdot \frac{1}{(Z^* - R)^2} = \frac{\beta}{(Z^* - R)^2} > 0$$
. Thus  $\tilde{R}$  is strictly convex in  $R$ .

**Inverse transformation:** Given a reward R used in the reverse KL objective J, what reward  $R_f$  should be used in the forward KL objective  $J_f$  to obtain the same optimal policy?

Setting  $\pi^*(y|x) = \pi_f^*(y|x)$ , we have  $\exp(R/\beta)/Z = \beta/(Z^* - R_f)$ , which simplifies to:

Reverse KL (with 
$$R$$
)  $\rightarrow$  Forward KL (with  $R_f$ ) 
$$R_f = Z^* - \beta Z \exp(-R/\beta)$$
 (135)

**Proposition I.2.** The function  $R_f = Z^* - \beta Z \exp(-R/\beta)$  is strictly monotonically increasing and strictly concave in R.

*Proof.* Taking first-order derivative  $\frac{\partial R_f}{\partial R} = -\beta Z \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{\beta}\right) \exp(-R/\beta) = Z \exp(-R/\beta) > 0$ ; therefore,  $R_f$  is strictly monotonically increasing (preserving reward ordering).

The second-order derivative is  $\frac{\partial^2 R_f}{\partial R^2} = Z \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{\beta}\right) \exp(-R/\beta) = -\frac{Z}{\beta} \exp(-R/\beta) < 0$ , we find that  $R_f$  is strictly concave (compressing high rewards).

|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|------------|-----------|-----------------------|
| Forward $KL \rightarrow Reverse KL$   | $-\beta \log(Z^* - R) + \text{const}$ | Yes        | Convex    | Amplify high rewards  |
| Reverse $KL \rightarrow$ Forward $KL$ | $Z^* - \beta Z e^{-R/\beta}$          | Yes        | Concave   | Compress high rewards |

# <span id="page-33-0"></span>I.2. Reward transformation for equivalent optima across f-divergences

We now generalize beyond KL divergence to arbitrary f-divergence regularization. Consider the objective:

$$J_f = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)}[R(x,y)] - \beta D_f(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x)||\pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x))$$
(136)

where the f-divergence is defined as  $D_f(P||Q) = \sum_y Q(y) f\left(\frac{P(y)}{Q(y)}\right)$  where  $f: \mathbb{R}^+ \to \mathbb{R}$  is a convex function with f(1) = 0. Standard KKT conditions yield the optimal policy:

$$\pi_f^*(y \mid x) = \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \cdot (f')^{-1} \left( \frac{R(x, y) - \lambda(x)}{\beta} \right)$$
(137)

where  $\lambda(x)$  is determined by the normalization constraint:

$$\sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \cdot (f')^{-1} \left( \frac{R(x,y) - \lambda(x)}{\beta} \right) = 1$$
 (138)

which was previously derived in (Wang et al., 2023).

Given two f-divergences with  $f_1$  and  $f_2$ , the optimal policies  $\pi_{f_1}^*$  and  $\pi_{f_2}^*$  coincide when:

$$(f_1')^{-1} \left( \frac{R_1 - \lambda_1}{\beta} \right) = (f_2')^{-1} \left( \frac{R_2 - \lambda_2}{\beta} \right)$$
 (139)

This yields the general reward transformation:

$$R_2 = \beta \cdot f_2' \left( (f_1')^{-1} \left( \frac{R_1 - \lambda_1}{\beta} \right) \right) + \lambda_2$$
 (140)

The curvature of this transformation is determined by the composition  $(f_2) \circ (f_1)^{-1}$ .

# <span id="page-35-0"></span>J. Policy Gradient Methods can be Derived from Sampled KL Estimators

If RL optimization is simply written as closing the reverse KL between current policy  $\pi_{\theta}$  and the reference policy distribution  $\pi_{\text{ref}}$  transformed by the return R (Peters & Schaal, 2007; Schulman et al., 2017a; Haarnoja et al., 2018), we have:

$$\min_{\theta} \text{KL}\left(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x) \Big| \Big| \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x) \exp\left(R(x,\cdot)\right)\right)$$
(141)

to make the connection with sampled KL estimators clear, we define the ratio between the optimal policy  $\pi^*$  and current policy  $\pi_{\theta}$  as w, same as before:

$$w = \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp R(x,y)}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x) Z(x)}$$
(142)

where the partition function Z(x) is approximated via a group of N trajectories per prompt  $y^{(i)} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)$  for  $i = 1, \dots, N$ :

$$\log Z(x) \triangleq \log \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)}[\exp R(x,y)] \approx \log \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}\right) \exp\left(R(x,y^{(i)})\right)$$
(143)

$$\geq \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( R(x, y^{(i)}) - \operatorname{sg}\left( \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)} \right) \right) \tag{144}$$

where the last step uses Jensen's inequality. We split it into two terms, value function (V(x)) and aggregate KL  $(\widehat{KL})$ :

$$V(x) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{y_{i=1,\dots,N}^{(i)} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} R(x, y^{(i)}) \right] \qquad \widehat{\mathrm{KL}} \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{y_{i=1,\dots,N}^{(i)} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathrm{sg} \left( \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)} \right) \right]$$
(145)

As a side note, to sharpen or flatten the target distribution, we simply need to multiply all rewards by  $1/\beta$ ; as a result, we omit  $\beta$  in our derivation and let it be absorbed by R(x, y).

Next, we show that every sampled KL estimator in Tab 1 can be turned into a policy gradient algorithm.

### <span id="page-35-1"></span>J.1. K1 estimator leads to on-policy PPO / GRPO loss

We start with  $\mathbf{K1} = -\log w$ :

K1: 
$$\min_{\theta} -\log w \qquad \nabla_{\theta} L_1 = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \left( R(x,y) - \log Z(x) \right)$$
$$= -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \left( R(x,y) - V(x) + \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \operatorname{sg} \left( \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)} \right) \right)$$

Summing over all N rollouts, we get

Therefore,  $L_1$  is simply a typical KL-regularized policy gradient objective:

K1: 
$$\min_{\theta} -\log w \quad \boxed{\nabla_{\theta} L_1 = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \Big( R(x, y) - V(x) \Big) + \nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x)) }$$
(147)

Which is what typical policy gradient algorithms like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017b) and GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) optimize in the on-policy case.

### <span id="page-36-0"></span>J.2. K2 estimator leads to APA / Kimi loss

Using the K2 estimator:

K2: 
$$\min_{\theta} \frac{1}{2} (\log w)^2 \qquad \qquad \nabla_{\theta} L_2 = \nabla_{\theta} \left( \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) - R(x,y) + \log Z(x) \right)^2$$

Expanding the partition function:

$$\nabla_{\theta} \bigg( \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) - (R(x,y) - V(x)) + \widehat{\text{KL}} \bigg)^{2}$$

$$= \underbrace{\nabla_{\theta} \bigg( \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) - (R(x,y) - V(x)) \bigg)^{2}}_{\text{APA Loss}} + \underbrace{\underbrace{\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \, \widehat{\text{KL}}}_{\text{Becomes } \nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)) \mid \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x))}_{\text{when summed over $N$ trajectories (Lemma J.1)}}$$

The loss  $L_2$  is the Advantage-induced Policy Alignment (APA) (Zhu et al., 2023), which was also used by Kimi models (Kimi et al., 2025b). The second term was often ignored, since prior works often approximate  $\log Z(x) \approx V(x)$  rather than  $\log Z(x) \approx V(x) - \widehat{\mathrm{KL}}$ . The APA loss already incorporates the same KL term:

$$\nabla_{\theta} (\log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) - (R(x,y) - V(x)))^{2} = \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) (R(x,y) - V(x)) + \nabla_{\theta} \text{KL}(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x) \| \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x))$$

Which means that the additional  $\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y \mid x) \widehat{KL}$  term just upweights the KL. Both APA and Kimi showed that this loss is effective in the off-policy regime without necessarily adding off-policy correction:

K2: 
$$\min_{\theta} \frac{1}{2} (\log w)^2 \qquad \boxed{\nabla_{\theta} L_2 = \nabla_{\theta} \Big( \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) - (R(x,y) - V(x)) \Big)^2}$$
 (148)

### <span id="page-37-0"></span>J.3. K5 estimator leads to RWR / MPO loss

Under the K5 estimator, the divergence minimization in policy optimization becomes:

$$\text{K5:} \quad \min_{\theta} \operatorname{sg}(w) \log w + \log r \qquad \nabla_{\theta} L_5 = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \left( \frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y|x) \, \exp R(x,y)}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x) \, Z(x)} \right)$$

Averaging over all N rollouts per x to simplify the partition function:

$$-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x) \left( \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x) \exp R(x, y^{(i)})}{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x) Z(x)} \right)$$

$$= -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x) \left( \frac{\operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}\right) \exp R(x, y^{(i)})}{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \operatorname{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(j)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(j)} \mid x)}\right) \exp R(x, y^{(j)})} \right)$$

$$= -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x) \cdot \operatorname{sg}\left( \frac{\exp\left(R(x, y^{(i)}) - \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} \exp\left(R(x, y^{(j)}) - \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(j)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(j)} \mid x)}\right)} \right)$$
softmax over  $[y^{(1)}, \dots, y^{(N)}]$ 

Which can be written in a batched manner over a group of N rollouts per prompt  $y = [y^{(1)}, \dots, y^{(N)}]$ , where mean and softmax are taken along the axis of the group dimension  $i = 1, \dots, N$  (which we denote as mean<sub>y</sub> and softmax<sub>y</sub>):

$$\operatorname{mean}_{y} \left( \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x}) \cdot \operatorname{softmax}_{y} \left( R(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}) - \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})}{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})} \right) \right)$$

This loss closely resembles Reward-Weighted Regression (**RWR**) (Peters & Schaal, 2007) and Maximum a Posteriori Policy Optimisation (**MPO**) (Abdolmaleki et al., 2018).

K5: 
$$\min_{\theta} \operatorname{sg}(w) \log w + \log r \quad \boxed{\nabla_{\theta} L_5 = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \cdot \operatorname{softmax}_y \Big( R(x,y) - \log(\pi_{\theta}(y|x)/\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y|x)) \Big)}$$
(149)

### <span id="page-37-1"></span>J.4. K3 / K3<sup>++</sup> / K4 estimators lead to combined losses

The K3 estimator leads to a combined  $L_1$  and  $L_5$  (GRPO + MPO):

K3: 
$$\min_{\theta} (-\log w + w - 1)$$

$$\nabla I = \nabla \log \pi (v|x) (R(x,y) - \log T(x)) \left( \nabla \log \pi (v|x) \right) \left( \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp R(x,y) \right)$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{3} = \underbrace{-\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) (R(x,y) - \log Z(x))}_{\text{Typical policy gradient, } L_{1}} - \underbrace{\left(\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)\right) \left(\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp R(x,y)}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x) Z(x)}\right)}_{\text{Same term as the one in } L_{5}} = \nabla_{\theta} L_{1} + \nabla_{\theta} L_{5} \quad (150)$$

The K3<sup>++</sup> estimator leads to a combined  $L_1$  and  $L_2$  (GRPO + APA):

$$K3^{++}: \min_{\theta} r \cdot (-\log w + w - 1) \qquad r = \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)}{\operatorname{sg}(\pi_{\theta}(y|x))}$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{3++} = \nabla_{\theta} L_{1} + \left(\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)\right) \left(\log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y|x) - R(x,y) + \log Z(x)\right) = \nabla_{\theta} L_{1} + \nabla_{\theta} L_{2} \quad (151)$$

The K4 estimator leads to  $L_2$  (same as the APA loss):

K4: 
$$\min_{\theta} r \cdot \operatorname{sg}(-\log w)$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} L_{4} = \left(\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)\right) \left(\log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) - \log \pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y|x) - R(x,y) + \log Z(x)\right) = \nabla_{\theta} L_{2}$$
(152)

A Note on Off-policy Correction Just like the off-policy correction for any sampled KL, any policy loss derived above can simply multiply a stop gradient version of the importance weight to remain unbiased when sampling is not on-policy:

$$y \sim \pi_{\text{sampling}}(\cdot \mid x)$$
  $s = \text{sg}\left(\frac{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{sampling}}(y \mid x)}\right)$ 

With the option of using additional PPO-like binary masks on the loss

$$M(x,y) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } R(x,y) - V(x) > 0 \text{ and } s > 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}} \\ 0 & \text{if } R(x,y) - V(x) < 0 \text{ and } s < 1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}} \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Leading us to conclude that the off-policy gradient under any loss function L above is

$$\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\text{sampling}}(\cdot|x)}[s(x,y) \, M(x,y) \, \nabla_{\theta} L(x,y)]$$

Though it is worth noting that many prior works find those losses to be effective in the absence of off-policy correction.

Summary of the Equivalence between Policy Gradient and Divergence Minimization We have shown that many existing policy gradient methods can be derived from sampled KL estimators. In other words, better techniques in sampled KL estimation may directly lead to better policy gradient algorithms under such an equivalence.

# <span id="page-39-0"></span>K. Policy Gradient as f-Divergence Minimization

Once a problem is framed as minimizing a KL, it can typically be recast into the general framework of f-divergence minimization, leading to a family of loss functions. We have seen this in a variety of other optimization problems: the generalization of GAN [\(Goodfellow et al.,](#page-8-19) [2020\)](#page-8-19) to f-GAN [\(Nowozin et al.,](#page-10-22) [2016\)](#page-10-22), GAIL [\(Ho & Ermon,](#page-9-21) [2016\)](#page-9-21) to f-MAX [\(Ghasemipour et al.,](#page-8-20) [2020\)](#page-8-20) and f-VIM [\(Ke et al.,](#page-9-22) [2020\)](#page-9-22), DPO [\(Rafailov et al.,](#page-10-14) [2023\)](#page-10-14) to f-DPO [\(Wang et al.,](#page-11-17) [2023\)](#page-11-17). In this section, we still assume that the optimal policy takes the form of a Boltzmann policy [\(Ziebart et al.,](#page-11-19) [2008;](#page-11-19) [Schulman et al.,](#page-10-20) [2017a\)](#page-10-20) due to its partition function having an intuitive interpretation (which says that the log partition function is equivalent to the value function), but explores using a sampled f-divergence estimator for policy optimization.

We now extend the results in [§J](#page-35-0) to generic f-divergence estimators. Let the optimal policy π <sup>∗</sup> be:

$$\pi^*(y|x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)}\pi_{\mathsf{ref}}(y|x)\exp(R(x,y)) \qquad w = \frac{\pi^*(y|x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)} = \frac{\pi_{\mathsf{ref}}(y|x)\,\exp{R(x,y)}}{\pi_{\theta}(y|x)\,Z(x)}$$

Re-iterating the definition of f-divergence, with f(·) being convex and f(1) = 0:

$$D_f(p||q) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim q} \left[ f\left(\frac{p(y)}{q(y)}\right) \right]$$

We now study two directions of f-divergence minimization: D<sup>f</sup> (πθ(·|x)||π ∗ (·|x)) and D<sup>f</sup> (π ∗ (·|x)||πθ(·|x)).

### K.1. Minimizing D<sup>f</sup> (πθ, π<sup>∗</sup> )

If we define function g<sup>f</sup> (·) as:

$$g_f(w) \triangleq w f\left(\frac{1}{w}\right) \tag{153}$$

Then, using the definition of f-divergence:

$$D_f(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)||\pi^*(\cdot|x)) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi^*(\cdot|x)} \left[ f\left(\frac{1}{w}\right) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ w f\left(\frac{1}{w}\right) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot|x)} \left[ g_f(w(x,y)) \right]$$
(154)

Note that w(x, y) has the following properties:

$$\nabla_{\theta} \log w(x, y) = -\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x), \qquad \nabla_{\theta} w(x, y) = -w(x, y) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x). \tag{155}$$

Therefore, using the score-function identity, we arrive at:

$$\nabla_{\theta} D_f(\pi_{\theta} \| \pi^*) = \nabla_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x)} \left[ g_f(w) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x)} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y | x) g_f(w) + \nabla_{\theta} g_f(w) \right]$$
$$= \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x)} \left[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y | x) \left( g_f(w) - w g_f'(w) \right) \right]. \tag{156}$$

Hence, minimizing D<sup>f</sup> (πθ∥π ∗ ) is equivalent to a policy gradient update with scalar weight −ϕ<sup>f</sup> (w) where ϕ<sup>f</sup> (w) is

$$\phi_f(w) \triangleq g_f(w) - w g_f'(w) = f'(1/w).$$
 (157)

### K.2. Minimizing D<sup>f</sup> (π ∗ , πθ)

We now consider the reverse direction:

$$D_f(\pi^*(\cdot|x) \parallel \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ f\left(\frac{\pi^*(y|x)}{\pi_\theta(y|x)}\right) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ f(w(x,y)) \right]. \tag{158}$$

By the same derivation as above,

$$\nabla_{\theta} D_f(\pi^* \| \pi_{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot | x)} \Big[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y | x) \left( f(w) - w f'(w) \right) \Big]. \tag{159}$$

Thus the induced policy-gradient weight for the reverse direction is

$$\psi_f(w) \triangleq f(w) - w f'(w). \tag{160}$$

Figure 10. Policy gradient based on  $\nabla_{\theta} D_f(\pi_{\theta}, \pi^*)$ , where the REINFORCE weight for  $\nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x)$  is  $-\phi_f(w) = -f'(1/w)$ .

**Summary of f-divergence loss.** The two directions of f-divergence minimization are reduced to the following:

$$\nabla_{\theta} D_f(\pi_{\theta}, \pi^*) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}} \Big[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \, \phi_f(w) \Big], \qquad \phi_f(w) = f'(1/w), \tag{161}$$

$$\nabla_{\theta} D_f(\pi^*, \pi_{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_{\theta}} \Big[ \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y|x) \, \psi_f(w) \Big], \qquad \psi_f(w) = f(w) - w f'(w). \tag{162}$$

**The EM Algorithm** The expectation-maximization (EM) process can be summarized as the following. For each prompt or task x, we sample N rollouts  $[y^{(1)}, \ldots, y^{(N)}]$  from  $\pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid x)$  to estimate the partition function Z(x) and the ratio w as:

$$\textbf{E-Step} \qquad Z(x) \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \operatorname{sg} \left( \frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y^{(i)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y^{(i)} \mid x)} \right) \exp \left( R(x, y^{(i)}) \right) \qquad w = \frac{\pi_{\operatorname{ref}}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta}(y \mid x)} \cdot \frac{\exp R(x, y)}{Z(x)}$$

<span id="page-40-0"></span>where the returns  $R(x, y^{(i)})$  are (pre-) scaled by  $1/\beta$  to control the regularization effect of the anchor  $\pi_{ref}$ . After the E-Step, M-Step performs gradient updates based on f-divergence minimization; the following two tables, Table 9 and Table 10, summarize what to minimize under each f-divergence (**M-Step**).
